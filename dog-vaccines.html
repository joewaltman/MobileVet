    • Vaccination Schedule for Dogs and Puppies

    "An ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure."

    Vaccines protect your dog from many life-threatening and untreatable diseases. Use this guide to help determine which vaccines your dog needs, and when he or she should get them.

    Veterinarians often split their vaccination recommendations into two groups: core and non-core vaccines. Core vaccines are recommended for all dogs and puppies in North America. Non-core vaccines are recommended on a case-by-case basis, depending on the lifestyle and local environment of your pet.

    Vaccines

    DAPP - Core

    This combination vaccine protects your dog from Distemper, Adenovirus type 1, Adenovirus Type 2, Parainfluenza, and Parvovirus. It should first be given at 6 weeks or older, then repeated every 3-4 weeks with the final dose being given between 14-16 weeks of age. If your dog or puppy is older than 16 weeks, one dose is considered protective for 12 months. At 12 months old, the vaccine should be given again. It is then considered protective for 3 years. The vaccine is also referred to as DA2PP or DHPP.

    Rabies - Core

    The rabies vaccine is given once when a puppy is 12 weeks or older. It should be repeated in 12 months. It is important to follow specific state, county and city legal requirements for rabies vaccination in your area. Contact VetPronto today for recommendations based on your local requirements.

    Bordetella - Non-Core

    This vaccine protects against Bordetella bronchiseptica, a bacteria that can cause a disease called infectious canine tracheobronchitis, or Kennel Cough. This is one of the most common communicable diseases among dogs. It is spread through contact with other infected dogs, and a dog is considered at high risk if they regularly go to dog parks, grooming, or boarding facilities. One dose should be given at 3-4 weeks or older, and it should be repeated every 12 months. In certain high risk situations, we recommend a booster of the Bordetella vaccine every 6 months.

    Leptospirosis - Non-Core

    This vaccine protects dogs from several different subspecies of Leptospira bacteria (canicola, grippotyphosa, icterohemaemorrhagiae, and pomona). The first dose should not be given until a puppy is 12 weeks or older. It needs to be boosted in 3-4 weeks, and then it is given annually in order to maintain protection. Dogs at highest risk are those that spend time outside and are exposed to areas frequented by wildlife. Small wild animals like raccoons, rats, and mice, are the natural reservoir for the bacteria, and it is transmitted through their urine.

    Lyme - Non-Core

    This vaccine protects against the bacteria that causes Lyme disease. It should only be given to dogs that are at a high risk for being exposed to ticks that transmit the disease. It should be given at 12 weeks or older, boostered in 3-4 weeks, and then given every year after that.

    Disease Descriptions

    Canine distemper

    Canine distemper is a viral disease that is highly contagious (via inhalation) and often fatal, making it the leading cause of infectious disease death in dogs. The virus infects the gastrointestinal tract, respiratory tract, brain, and spinal cord. Common symptoms include high fever, watery discharge from the eyes and nose, vomiting and diarrhea, hardening of the footpads and nose, seizures, and paralysis.

    Infectious Canine Hepatitis

    This contagious disease of dogs is caused by Canine Adenovirus Type 1. It most often presents with signs that vary from a slight fever and congestion of the mucous membranes to severe depression, marked leukopenia, coagulation disorders, and death. The primary routes of infection are through the ingestion of urine, feces, or saliva from infected dogs. The disease has a mortality rate of 10% - 30%, and it is most severe in young puppies.

    Canine Parvovirus Infection

    This viral disease is caused most often by infection with canine parvovirus type 2b. “Parvo” is highly contagious and spreads from dog to dog through either direct contact or indirect fecal-oral transmission. The more common form of the disease is the intestinal presentation, which is characterized by vomiting, diarrhea, weight loss, and lack of appetite. The less common form is the cardiac type, which attacks the heart muscles of very young puppies and often leads to death. Most cases are seen in puppies that are between six weeks and six months old. Mortality can exceed 90% in untreated cases, and treatment often involves hospitalization.

    Canine Parainfluenza

    Canine parainfluenza virus is a highly contagious respiratory virus. It is one of the most common pathogens (along with Bordetella) involved in canine infectious tracheobronchitis, also known as kennel cough. It is usually transmitted through the air and spreads rapidly in kennels or shelters where large numbers of dogs are kept together.

    Rabies

    Rabies is a viral disease that causes acute inflammation of the brain in dogs, cats, humans, and other warm-blooded animals. It is usually transmitted in saliva through the bite of an infected animal but can also spread through contact with a wound or mucosal surface of the eyes, nose, or mouth. Early symptoms can include fever and tingling at the site of exposure, but the disease progresses to include one or more of the following: violent movements, uncontrolled excitement, fear of water, an inability to move parts of the body, confusion, and loss of consciousness. The disease can only be diagnosed after an animal starts to show symptoms, and there is no treatment. Rabies almost always results in death.

    Lyme disease

    Lyme disease is a common tick-borne disease caused by infection with the bacteria, Borrelia burgdorferi, and transmitted by hard-shelled deer ticks. Infection typically occurs after the infected tick has been attached to a dog for at least 18 hours. The most common clinical presentation is reccurring lameness caused by inflammation of the joints. This is sometimes called “shifting leg lameness” because of the way it can move from one affected leg to another.

    Canine Infectious Tracheobronchitis

    This disease, also called kennel cough, is one of the most common infectious diseases among dogs. It is spread through contact with other infected dogs, and a dog is considered at high risk if they regularly go to dog parks, grooming, or boarding facilities. The disease is characterized by an inflammation of the trachea and bronchi, and it is most often caused by infection with Bordetella bronchiseptica, parainfluenza virus, or mycoplasma. The classic presentation is a harsh, honking cough. Most dogs are able to clear the infection on their own, but young puppies, older dogs, and pregnant females are at a higher risk of more serious complications.

    Leptospirosis

    Leptospirosis results from infection by one or more or several subspecies of Leptospira bacteria. The bacteria invade through the skin and proceed to spread through the bloodstream and reproduce in many of the dog’s vital organs. The most common initial signs of infection are fever and other general symptoms associated with illness (weakness, depression, shivering, loss of appetite, sore muscles). These symptoms resolve in many dogs, but the bacteria continues to live in the kidneys and liver where it can cause chronic disease and eventually death. Leptospirosis is also a zoonotic disease, and children are most at risk of acquiring the bacteria from an infected pet.

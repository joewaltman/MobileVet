    "An ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure."

    Vaccines prevent many diseases that are life threatening and cannot be treated. Use this guide to help you know what vaccines your cat needs.

    Feline vaccines are separated into two groups, core and non-core vaccines. Core vaccines are recommended for all cats or kittens in North America, and non-core vaccines are optional and are recommended depending on the lifestyle and environment of your cat or kitten.

    Vaccines

    FVCRP - Core

    This is known as the ‘core’ cat vaccine and contains vaccines for Feline Viral rhinotracheitis, Feline Calicivirus, and Feline Panleukopenia. This vaccine should be started at 8-9 weeks or older but can be given as early as 6 weeks if there is a high risk. This vaccine is then repeated every 3-4 weeks. The final dose should be given between 16-20 weeks of age. If the cat is over 16 weeks when it receives this vaccine for the first timem, it should be boosted once in 3-4 weeks, and again in 12 months. After that, this vaccine should be given at least once every 3 years to stay current.

    Rabies - Core

    The rabies vaccine is given once when a kitten is 12 weeks or older. It should be boosted in 12 months. It is important to follow specific state/county and city legal requirements for rabies vaccine in your area. Contact a vet today if you are not sure of your requirements.

    FeLV - Non-Core

    FeLV is considered optional as it is only transmitted by direct contact with other cats so only outdoor cats can be exposed and is controversial whether or not indoor cats should receive this vaccine as there have been soft tissue cell sarcomas associated with this vaccine in rare cases, which oftentimes requires limb amputation to treat and results in death. This vaccine should be given once at 9 weeks or older and repeated in 3-4 weeks. It should then have a booster after 12 months. After the booster, it is recommended to booster once every 12 months if the cat is at high risk of exposure to this disease.

    Disease Descriptions

    Feline leukemia virus (FeLV)

    FeLV is a retrovirus transmitted between infected cats when the transfer of saliva or nasal secretions is involved, for example when sharing a feeding dish. If not defeated by the animal's immune system, the virus can be lethal. If administered, it should be injected in tip of the left rear paw (unless combined with rabies).

    Feline viral rhinotracheitis (FVR)

    FVR is an upper respiratory infection of cats, also known as feline influenza, caused by feline herpesvirus 1 (FHV-1). FVR is very contagious and can cause severe disease, including death from pneumonia in young kittens.

    Feline calicivirus (FCV)

    In addition to FHV-1, FCV is the other common viral cause of respiratory infection in cats.

    Feline panleukopenia virus (FPV)

    FPV, more commonly known as feline distemper, is caused the feline parvovirus, a close relative of canine parvovirus. It is not related to canine distemper. Panleukopenia is highly contagious and can be fatal.

    Rabies

    Rabies is a viral disease that causes acute inflammation of the brain in humans and other warm-blooded animals. Early symptoms can include fever and tingling at the site of exposure. These symptoms are followed by one or more of the following symptoms: violent movements, uncontrolled excitement, fear of water, an inability to move parts of the body, confusion, and loss of consciousness. After symptoms appear, rabies almost always results in death. The time period between contracting the disease and the start of symptoms is usually one to three months. However, this time period can vary from less than one week to more than one year. The time period depends on the distance the virus must travel to reach the central nervous system.

    Rabies is transmitted to humans from other animals. Rabies can be transmitted when an infected animal scratches or bites another animal or human. Saliva from an infected animal can also transmit rabies if the saliva comes into contact with a mucous membrane of another animal or human. Most rabies cases in humans are the result of dog bites. More than 99% of rabies cases in countries where dogs commonly have rabies are caused by dog bites. In the Americas, bats are the most common cause of rabies, and less than 5% of rabies cases in humans are from dogs. Rodents are very rarely infected with rabies. The rabies virustravels to the brain by following the peripheral nerves. The disease can only be diagnosed after the start of symptoms.

    Animal control and vaccination programs have decreased the risk of rabies from dogs in a number of regions of the world. Immunizing people before they are exposed is recommended for those who are at high risk. The high-risk group includes people who work with bats or who spend prolonged periods in areas of the world where rabies is common. In people who have been exposed to rabies, the rabies vaccine and sometimes rabies immunoglobulin are effective in preventing the disease if the person receives the treatment before the start of rabies symptoms. Washing bites and scratches for 15 minutes with soap and water, povidone iodine, or detergent as they may kill the virus also appears to be somewhat effective at preventing rabies transmission. Only a few people have survived a rabies infection and this was with extensive treatment, known as the Milwaukee protocol.

    Rabies causes about 26,000 to 55,000 deaths worldwide per year. More than 95% of these deaths occur in Asia and Africa. Rabies is present in more than 150 countries and on all continents but Antarctica. More than 3 billion people live in regions of the world where rabies occurs. In most of Europe and Australia, rabies is only present in bats. Many small island nations do not have rabies at all.
